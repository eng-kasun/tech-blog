## The Wood Wide Web

Beneath every forest floor lies an invisible internet. Not made of fiber optics, but of **mycelium** — vast networks of fungal threads that connect trees, share nutrients, and transmit chemical signals across entire ecosystems. Scientists call it the "Wood Wide Web," and its sophistication is staggering.

## How It Works

Mycelium forms **mycorrhizal associations** with plant roots. The fungi extend hair-thin hyphae far beyond what roots alone can reach, accessing water and minerals — especially phosphorus — in exchange for sugars that the tree produces through photosynthesis. It's a mutualistic trade network operating at a massive scale.

A single cubic centimeter of forest soil can contain up to 8 kilometers of mycelial threads. A single fungal network can span hectares, connecting hundreds of trees across species boundaries.

## Resource Sharing and Communication

Research by Dr. Suzanne Simard and others has revealed that trees use mycelial networks to:

- **Share carbon** — Mother trees send sugars to struggling seedlings, especially their own offspring, through fungal connections
- **Transfer warnings** — When a tree is attacked by insects, it sends chemical signals through the mycelium that trigger defensive responses in neighboring trees
- **Redistribute nutrients** — Dying trees dump their nutrients into the network, effectively bequeathing their resources to the community

## Is It Intelligence?

The mycelium network exhibits behaviors remarkably similar to biological neural networks. The slime mold *Physarum polycephalum* (a related organism) can solve mazes, optimize transport networks, and even replicate the Tokyo rail system when given food sources at station locations.

Mycelial networks make "decisions" about resource allocation, adapt to environmental changes, and exhibit a form of memory. Whether this constitutes intelligence depends on your definition, but it's undeniably a sophisticated form of biological information processing.

## Implications for Science and Technology

Understanding mycelium is inspiring breakthroughs in:

- **Sustainable materials** — Mycelium-based packaging, building insulation, and even leather alternatives are entering the market
- **Environmental cleanup** — Mycoremediation uses fungi to break down pollutants, including oil spills and heavy metals
- **Network design** — Mycelial growth patterns are being studied to optimize human infrastructure networks

## A Different Kind of Life

The mycelium network challenges our plant-centric view of forests. Trees aren't isolated individuals competing for light — they're nodes in a vast, cooperative network, facilitated by fungi that have been doing distributed computing for 400 million years.

The forest floor isn't just dirt. It's the most sophisticated biological network on Earth.
